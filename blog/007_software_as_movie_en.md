## **🎞️ Software as a Feature-Film Screenplay**
 
The emergence of Artificial Intelligence such as ChatGPT, Claude, or GitHub Copilot has fundamentally changed software development. The old rule of thumb “read ten times more than you write” applies today more than ever — except that by now we no longer read our own code, but primarily code generated by machines. To be able to even grasp and control this flood as humans, we need a new perspective on software architecture.

Computers process arbitrary data structures. Humans, on the other hand, understand the world through stories. Biologically speaking, our brain is programmed to store information through cause, effect, and **emotions**. Where naked data tends to create cognitive coldness and is quickly forgotten, good stories create resonance. They arouse curiosity, create empathy for the domain processes, and motivate the reader to continue. That is why it makes sense to design code like a film screenplay.[ ](https://github.com/andreas-wagner-dev/object-oriented-learning-journey/blob/main/blog/picture/008_00_code_like_movie.png)

In contrast to approaches that view software as a technical layered landscape of stateless services with passive data containers, the feature-film philosophy stages an object-oriented system as a living ensemble of intelligent actors. These actors appear in scenes, act in acts, and give the logical sequence a humanly tangible dramaturgy. The story is thus no longer merely executed, but made emotionally experienceable through the behavior of the actors.

### 1. **The screenplay as a blueprint**
 
Modeling an object-oriented system takes place via three central graphs. In this context, the screenplay assumes the role of a blueprint: it defines the framework in which the static structure and the fleeting moment of interaction seamlessly interlock.[ ](https://github.com/andreas-wagner-dev/object-oriented-learning-journey/blob/main/blog/picture/008_01_code_like_movie.png)

- **The construction graph (the directing / the set setup):** It describes instantiation and the build-up of the object scaffolding by the [Composition Root](https://blog.ploeh.dk/2011/07/28/CompositionRoot/) (Who owns whom?). Here the “stage engineering” is installed before the first scene begins.
- **The collaboration graph (the ensemble):** It defines the permanent relationships between the objects (Who knows whom and can communicate with each other?). This corresponds to the fixed casting of roles and their networking with one another.
- **The invocation graph (the plot):** It maps the dynamic runtime interactions (Who is calling which method right now?) — the actual dialogue between the actors during the execution of a scene.

**The central directing instructions**

The **Law of Demeter** and the **Tell Don't Ask** principle function as central directing instructions. The Law of Demeter prevents transitive coupling by ensuring that an object interacts only with its direct collaboration partners. The Tell‑Don't‑Ask principle ensures that behavior is executed where it semantically belongs, instead of carrying internal state outward. This creates a lively, behavior-oriented structure that touches us emotionally, instead of a procedural query script that quickly feels monotonous.

**The table of contents of the screenplay**

To ensure a domain-oriented and progressive flow of information, the table of contents of the screenplay must map a clear navigation path that helps the reader locate the components of the story efficiently. Three pragmatic rules apply:

- **Clear dependencies:** Packages must never depend on their subpackages. Cyclic references are strictly forbidden to preserve the directed information flow.
- **Precise detail refinement:** Subpackages do not introduce new concepts. They merely concretize already existing terms of the parent packages.
- **Living domain language:** Packages mirror the language of the domain (Ubiquitous Language), not the technical terms of the implementers.

**Components and dependencies of the staging**

To tell the story without plot holes, the packages follow a strict hierarchy:

**Ideas & Concepts (Domain):** The conceptual foundation. Here are the central ideas and domain rules. This layer is completely autonomous and depends on nothing. All other packages build on it.

**Backstage (Infrastructure):** The technical infrastructure. Here are tools, databases, and APIs. It serves the ensemble and may depend only on the domain.

**Scenes (Details):** The domain detail packages. They fill abstract domain concepts with life. Here, concrete sequences and interactions of the story are shaped.

**Stage (UI):** The visible surface. Here, what happens is made visible to the audience (the users). The stage accesses domain, scenes, and infrastructure.

**Directing (Composition Root):** The directing package functions as the operational control center. In a composition root class, it provides the central entry point main() and orchestrates the interplay of all classes. Here, decisions are made about instantiation of objects and all settings required at startup. Directing is thus the only instance that knows the entire ensemble and assembles the various components into a finished staging. No other package may depend on directing.

#### **3.1. The actor (Actor) – the central interface**
 
In the world of film there are stars, extras, and walk-ons. In the screenplay all play the same role: they are actors who perform something and communicate with one another. This domain unity is expressed in code via a central interface:

```java
public interface Actor {
 Name name();
 void perform();
 void speak(Media media);
}
```

Every Actor can act (perform), communicate (speak), and has a name (name). These three core abilities are universal — whether lead, extra, or walk-on. The expression of their role comes through concrete implementations, not through technical categorizations.

#### **3.2 The prop (Name) – the precise domain value**
 
In film, props have a clear meaning: they are not interchangeable objects. A wedding ring is not “some object,” but a symbol with emotional and functional meaning.

The same applies to domain values in code. A name is not a primitive string, but a precise domain value that carries semantic validation and identity:

```java
public class Name {
 private String value;
 public Name(String value) {
 if (value == null \
\
 value.trim().isEmpty()) {
 throw new IllegalArgumentException("Name cannot be empty!");
 }
 this.value = value;
 }
 @Override
 public String toString() { return value; }
 public String value() { return value; }
}
```

Through this encapsulation, the value receives a domain identity. The constructor already validates at creation time that the name cannot be empty. This defensive strategy prevents faulty states before they can spread through the system. What technically looks like a wrapper is functionally a guarantor of integrity and expressiveness.

#### **3.3 The costume (Outfit) – the base decorator**
 
Actors wear different costumes in a film. A suit conveys elegance; a uniform conveys authority. In code, the **Decorator Pattern** fulfills the same purpose: it extends actors with additional behavior without changing their identity.

```java
/**
 * Abstract outfit to decorate an actor.
 */
public abstract class Outfit implements Actor {
 
 protected final Actor decoratedActor;
 
 protected Outfit(Actor actor) { this.decoratedActor = actor; }
 
 @Override
 public Name name() { return decoratedActor.name(); }
 
 @Override
 public void perform() { decoratedActor.perform(); }
}
```

The base class Outfit is an abstract decorator. It holds a reference to the decorated actor and delegates the central methods name() and perform() directly to it. This guarantees that the basic identity of the actor remains unchanged. Concrete subclasses can add specific behavior before, after, or instead of delegation. Thus an actor is equipped with additional abilities without changing its core. The speak(Media) method is deliberately not automatically delegated, because each concrete outfit should decide itself how it communicates.

#### **3.4 The scene (Scene) – the interaction protocol**
 
A scene is the smallest coherent plot building block of a film. It describes a self-contained interaction between actors at a place and time.

```java
@FunctionalInterface 
public interface Scene {
 void execute(); 
}
```

This interface reduces the scene to its essence: executing an action. Thanks to the @FunctionalInterface annotation it can also be used as a lambda or method reference. A scene does not say _how_ the action happens, only _that_ it is executed. All details remain up to the concrete implementation.

#### **3.5 The medium (Media) – the information carrier**
 
Communication in films happens through various media: speech, gestures, letters, phone calls. In code, Media represents the carrier of this communication.

The Media interface defines a fluent API to populate content.

```java
public interface Media {
 Media with(String name, String value);
 Media with(String name, boolean value);
 public String content(String name);
}
```

The class Media.Default provides a standard implementation that stores content in a map.

```java
public abstract class Default implements Media {
 
 protected final Map<String, Object> content = new HashMap<>(0);
 @Override
 public Media with(String name, String value) {
 content.put(name, value);
 return this;
 }
 
 @Override
 public Media with(String name, boolean value) {
 content.put(name, value);
 return this;
 }
 
 public String content(String name) {
 return content.getOrDefault(name, "").toString();
 }
 
 @Override
 public String toString() { return content.toString(); }
}
```

Air is a concrete medium for invisible transmission — for example the spoken word into the air.

```java
public final class Air extends Media.Default { } 
```

Further specializations can be added for different contexts, such as Email, RecordMedia, or VideoCanvas.

#### **3.6 The film (Movie) – the red thread**
 
The film is the Gesamtkunstwerk. It orchestrates the scenes and defines the flow of the story.

```java
public interface Movie {
 void play(); 
}
```

The Movie interface is deliberately minimalistic: a single method play() starts the staging. The concrete order of scenes, their chaining, and logic is defined in an implementation such as AnalogMovie. This keeps the domain abstraction (“play the film”) clearly separated from technical orchestration.

The heart of every story are the characters. They carry the plot, represent domain roles, and act as autonomous actors with clear behavior.[ ](https://github.com/andreas-wagner-dev/object-oriented-learning-journey/blob/main/blog/picture/008_04_code_like_movie.png)

```
├─ Guests.java ← The guests (guest group as an ensemble)
```

All characters implement the central Actor interface and represent their domain role through specific behavior. In contrast to passive data containers, they decide themselves about their actions and communication.

#### **4.1 The bride (Bride)**
 
The bride is one of the central protagonists of the wedding story. She can recite her vows, exchange rings, and declare her consent.

```java
public class Bride implements Actor {
	private final Name name;
	private boolean hasRing = false;
	public Bride(String name) {
		this.name = new Name(name);
	}
	@Override
	public Name name() { return name;}
	@Override
	public void perform() { System.out.println("💍 " + name + " recites her wedding vows.."); }
	public void giveRing(Media ringBasket) {
		ringBasket.with("groom-ring", "Wedding-Ring");
		System.out.println("💍 " + name + " hands over " + ringBasket + ".");
	}
	public void receiveRing(Media ringBasket) {
		String ring = ringBasket.content("bride-ring");
		if (ring.isEmpty()) {
			throw new IllegalStateException("Wedding ring is missing!");
		}
		if (hasRing) {
			throw new IllegalStateException("The bride is already wearing a ring!");
		}
		System.out.println("💍 " + name + " take the " + ring + ".");
		hasRing = true;
	}
	public void speak(Media media) {
		media.with("role", "Bride")
		.with("name", name.value())
		.with("text", "Yes, I will...")
		.with("hasRing", hasRing);
	}
} 
```

The bride manages her own state (hasRing) and makes decisions independently. The receiveRing method defensively checks whether a ring is already present. This keeps business logic where it semantically belongs: with the bride herself, not in an external service.

#### **4.2 The groom (Groom)**
 
The groom plays the complementary role to the bride. His implementation follows the same principle: he is an autonomous actor with his own identity and behavior.

```java
public class Groom implements Actor {
	private final Name name;
	private boolean hasRing;
	public Groom(String name) {
		this.name = new Name(name);
	}
	@Override
	public Name name() { return name;}
	@Override
	public void perform() { System.out.println("💍 " + name + " recites her wedding vows.."); }
	public void giveRing(Media ringBasket) {
		ringBasket.with("bride-ring", "Wedding-Ring");
		System.out.println("💍 " + name + " presents the " + ringBasket + ".");
	}
	
	public void receiveRing(Media ringBasket) {
		String ring = ringBasket.content("groom-ring");
		if (ring.isEmpty()) {
			throw new IllegalStateException("Wedding ring is missing!");
		}
		if (hasRing) {
			throw new IllegalStateException("The groom is already wearing a ring!");
		}
		System.out.println("💍 " + name + " take the " + ring + ".");
		hasRing = true;
	}
	
	public void speak(Media media) {
		media.with("role", "Groom")
		.with("name", name.value())
		.with("text", "Yes, I will...")
		.with("hasRing", hasRing);
	}
} 
```

Bride and Groom are structurally similar, but not identical. Each character has its own perspective on what happens. This duplication is intentional: it preserves semantics and allows both roles to evolve independently, without changes to one automatically affecting the other.

#### **4.3 The newborn (Newborn)**
 
With the birth, a new character enters the stage: the newborn. It represents the next generation and extends the ensemble.

#### **4.4 The guest (Guest)**
 
Guests are supporting characters who enrich the wedding. They can applaud and congratulate.

```java
public class Guest implements Actor {
 private final Name name;
 public Guest(String name) {
 this.name = new Name(name);
 }
 @Override
 public Name name() { return name; }
 @Override
 public void perform() {
		System.out.println("🧍‍♂️" + name + " smiles.");
 }
 public void clap() {
 Media.Air air = new Media.Air();
 speak(air);
 System.out.println(air.toString());
 }
 public void speak(Media media) {
 media.with("role", "Guest")
 .with("name", name.value())
 .with("text", "💬 " + name + " congratulations!")
 .with("action", "👏 " + name + " applaud!");
 }
} 
```

The clap() method shows how a guest stages its own communication: it creates an Air medium, speaks into it, and prints it. This is an example of autonomous behavior that requires no external coordination.

#### **4.5 The guest group (Guests)**
 
Multiple guests together form a group. This is modeled as an iterable collection so all guests can be traversed easily.

```java
public class Guests implements Iterable<Guest> {
 private List<Guest> guests;
 public Guests(List<Guest> guests) {
 this.guests = new ArrayList<>(guests);
 }
 @Override
 public Iterator<Guest> iterator() {
 return guests.iterator();
 }
 public Guests add(Guest guest) {
 List<Guest> newList = new ArrayList<>(guests);
 newList.add(guest);
 return new Guests(newList);
 }
} 
```

The Guests class is deliberately designed immutable. The add method creates a new instance with the added guest instead of modifying the existing list. This preserves data integrity and avoids side effects.

Musicians play an important role at the wedding. They provide musical accompaniment.

#### **4.7 The band (Band)**
 
Multiple musicians together form a band. This group has its own name and can be iterated.

```java
public class Band implements Iterable<Musician> {
 private List<Musician> group;
 private final Name name;
 public Band(String name, List<Musician> group) {
 this.name = new Name(name);
 this.group = new ArrayList<>(group);
 }
 public Name name() { return name; }
 @Override
 public Iterator<Musician> iterator() {
 return group.iterator();
 }
 public Band add(Musician guest) {
 List<Musician> newList = new ArrayList<>(group);
 newList.add(guest);
 return new Band(name.value(), newList);
 }
} 
```

The band itself has a name and groups musicians. As with Guests, immutability is preserved here as well: add creates a new Band instance instead of modifying the existing one.

### **5. The makeup (the structural wrappers)**
 
In the world of film, makeup transforms actors into their roles. A suit turns a performer into a gentleman; a costume gives authority or elegance. In code, the **Decorator Pattern** fulfills the same purpose: it equips actors with additional behavior without changing their core identity.[ ](https://github.com/andreas-wagner-dev/object-oriented-learning-journey/blob/main/blog/picture/008_05_code_like_movie.png)

```
manuscript/outfit/
│
├─ ActorInSuite.java ← Actor in a suit (visual costume)
├─ BrideWithArchive.java ← Bride with database access
├─ BrideWithMailbox.java ← Bride with email functionality
├─ GroomWithCard.java ← Groom with payment card
└─ GroomWithPhone.java ← Groom with phone
```

Each outfit extends an actor with specific behavior. This happens through composition instead of inheritance: a decorated actor delegates the core functionality to the wrapped actor and adds only selectively new abilities.

#### **5.1 Actor in a suit (ActorInSuite)**
 
The suit is a purely visual costume. It changes how the actor steps onto the stage without changing its abilities.

```java
public class ActorInSuite extends Outfit {
 
 public ActorInSuite(Actor actor) {
 super(actor);
 }
 
 @Override
 public void perform() {
 System.out.println("🤵 " + name() + " steps onto the stage in a wedding suit.");
 decoratedActor.perform();
 }
 @Override
 public void speak(Media media) {
 decoratedActor.speak(media);
 }
} 
```

ActorInSuite overrides perform() and adds a visual hint (“steps onto the stage in a wedding suit”) before executing the actor’s original action. The speak method is delegated directly. This shows how behavior is extended in a targeted way without redefining all functionality.

#### **5.2 Bride with database access (BrideWithArchive)**
 
Through this decorator, the bride gains the ability to store her data in a database. This is an example of separating domain logic (bride) from technical infrastructure (database).

```java
public class BrideWithArchive extends Outfit {
 private Archive arhive;
 public BrideWithArchive(Bride bride, Archive arhive) {
 super(bride);
 this.arhive = arhive;
 }
 
 @Override
 public void speak(Media spaek) { decoratedActor.speak(spaek); }
 
 @Override
 public void perform() {
 // perform the default behavier
 decoratedActor.perform();
 // Simulate database storage
 // The Archive hands the Actor a blank sheet (RecordMedia).
 RecordMedia record = new RecordMedia();
 // The actor "speaks" his data onto the sheet.
 speak(record);
 arhive.store(record);
 System.out.println("💾 " + name() + " was saved in the database.");
 }
} 
```

BrideWithArchive adds a database operation after the original action (decoratedActor.perform()). The actor “speaks” its data into a RecordMedia object, which is then stored in the Archive. This choreography shows how infrastructure can be embedded seamlessly into domain action without the core logic of the bride having to know about it.

#### **5.3 Bride with email functionality (BrideWithMailbox)**
 
This decorator equips the bride with the ability to send invitations by email.

```java
public class BrideWithMailbox extends Outfit {
 private EmailBox emailBox;
 public BrideWithMailbox(Bride bride, EmailBox emailBox) {
 super(bride);
 this.emailBox = emailBox;
 }
 
 @Override
 public void speak(Media spaek) {
 decoratedActor.speak(spaek);
 }
 
 public void invite(Actor actor) {
 emailBox.put(new Email(name().value(), actor.name().value(), "Invitation to Wedding..."));
 }
} 
```

The invite method is new and uses EmailBox to send an invitation. The bride herself remains unchanged; only the decorated version gets this additional ability. This shows the strength of the Decorator Pattern: behavior can be added selectively and contextually.

#### **5.4 Groom with payment card (GroomWithCard)**
 
Through this decorator, the groom gains the ability to make payments. It additionally implements the Payer interface to make the payment function explicit.

```java
public class GroomWithCard extends Outfit implements Payer {
 private StripeApi stripeApi;
 public GroomWithCard(Actor actor, StripeApi stripeApi) {
 super(actor);
 this.stripeApi = stripeApi;
 }
 @Override
 public void speak(Media spaek) {
 decoratedActor.speak(spaek);
 }
 @Override
 public void pay(double amount, String toAccountId) {
 System.out.println("💰 " + name() + " Initialize payment of €" + amount);
 String groomAccountId = "stripe_account_" + name().value().toLowerCase();
 stripeApi.execute(new Transaction(groomAccountId, toAccountId), amount);
 }
} 
```

The pay method translates the domain action (“the groom pays”) into a technical API operation. The decorator acts here as an **adapter**: it encapsulates the details of the Stripe API and exposes them through a domain interface.

#### **5.5 Groom with phone (GroomWithPhone)**
 
This decorator equips the groom with a phone via which he can make and receive calls.

```java
public class GroomWithPhone extends Outfit implements Headset {
 private Phone phone;
 public GroomWithPhone(Actor actor, Phone phone) {
 super(actor);
 this.phone = phone;
 this.phone.receive(this);
 }
 public void call(String message) {
 System.out.println("📞 Calling the Band 🎼...");
 phone.publish(message);
 }
 @Override
 public void accept(String message) {
 System.out.println("📞 Confirmation from the band 🎹: " + message);
 }
 @Override
 public void speak(Media spaek) {
 decoratedActor.speak(spaek);
 }
} 
```

By implementing the Headset interface, the groom can be registered as a recipient of messages. The call method sends a message via the Phone, while accept processes incoming responses. This is an example of asynchronous communication that is seamlessly integrated into the object model.

### **6. Stage engineering (the infrastructure layer)**
 
While actors and scenes carry the domain essence of the story, stage engineering works in the background. It ensures that lighting, sound, and sets function — but remains invisible to the audience. In code, this corresponds to the infrastructure layer: databases, APIs, communication channels, and payment services.[ ](https://github.com/andreas-wagner-dev/object-oriented-learning-journey/blob/main/blog/picture/008_06_code_like_movie.png)

```

...
├─ archive/
│ ├─ DatabaseArchive.java ← Concrete database connection
│ └─ RecordMedia.java ← Data carrier for database entries
├─ email/
│ ├─ Email.java ← Email data object
│ └─ EmailBox.java ← Email sending adapter
├─ payment/
│ ├─ StripeApi.java ← Adapter to the Stripe payment API
│ └─ Transaction.java ← Transaction object for payments
├─ phone/
│ ├─ Phone.java ← Phone communication adapter
```

Stage engineering is strictly separated from the domain. All infrastructure components are abstracted via interfaces so the domain logic has no direct dependencies on technical implementations. This makes it possible to swap databases, APIs, or communication pathways without changing the core story.

#### **6.1 The archive (Archive & DatabaseArchive)**
 
The archive stores data permanently. In film, that would be the film roll that is developed later. In code, it is the database.

**Interface:**

```java
public interface Archive {
 void store(RecordMedia media);
} 
```

**Implementation:**

```java
public class DatabaseArchive implements Archive {
 
 private final String connectionString;
 
 public DatabaseArchive(String connectionString) {
 this.connectionString = connectionString;
 System.out.println("💾 Connect database: " + connectionString);
 }
 
 @Override
 public void store(RecordMedia media) {
 // Simulate database manipulation operation
 System.out.println("💾 Storing in database: " + media.toDML());
 }
} 
```

The Archive interface defines the domain action: store. The concrete implementation DatabaseArchive handles the technical implementation. This keeps the domain independent of the database choice.

**The recording medium (RecordMedia):**

```java
public class RecordMedia extends Media.Default {
 
 public String toDML() {
 // Simulate build DML statement
 return content.toString();
 }
} 
```

RecordMedia is a specialized medium for database entries. It inherits from Media.Default and adds a method to bring content into a storable format.

#### **6.2 The mail (PostBox, Email & EmailBox)**
 
Mail is a communication channel. In film, those would be letters; in code, emails.

**Interface:**

```java
public interface PostBox<T> {
 void put(T content);
} 
```

The generic interface PostBox<T> abstracts the dispatch mechanism. It knows only the action “put something,” not the type of content.

**Email data object:**

```java
public class Email {
 private String from;
 private String to;
 private String content;
 public Email(String from, String to, String content) {
 this.from = from;
```

#### **6.4 The phone (Phone, Headset & Channel)**
 
The phone enables asynchronous communication between actors. It follows the **Observer Pattern**: messages are published, and registered recipients are notified.

**Communication channel:**

```java
public interface Channel<T> {
	void subscribe(Consumer<T> subscriber);
	void publish(T message);
} 
```

The Channel interface abstracts a publish/subscribe mechanism. Headsets can register and are notified on new messages.

**Phone implementation:**

```java
public class Phone implements Channel<String> {
	private String phoneNummer;
	private List<Consumer<String>> subscribers = new ArrayList<>(0);
	public Phone(String phoneNummer) {
		this.phoneNummer = phoneNummer;
		System.out.println("📞 Phone ready: " + this.phoneNummer);
	}
	@Override
	public void subscribe(Consumer<String> subscriber) {
		this.subscribers.add(subscriber);
	}
	@Override
	public void publish(String message) {
		System.out.println("📞 Sending message: " + message);
		subscribers.forEach(subscriber -> subscriber.accept(message));
	}
} 
```

The Phone manages a list of headset recipients. When publishing a message, all registered recipients are notified. This shows how asynchronous communication can be modeled in an object-oriented way without relying on message brokers or event systems.

**Interface for recipients:**

```java
public interface Headset extends Consumer<String> {
 void accept(String message);
} 
```

The Headset interface defines a recipient that can accept messages.

**Video flashback with Proxy Pattern**

The Proxy Pattern is used here to delay loading of a resource-intensive video until it is actually needed.

**Video interface:**

```java
public interface Video {
 void show(Media canvas);
} 
```

**Real video (RealVideo):**

```java
public class RealVideo implements Video {
 private String filename;
 public RealVideo(String filename) {
 this.filename = filename;
 load();
 }
 private void load() {
 System.out.println("⏳ Loading heavy video file: " + filename + "...");
 try {
 Thread.sleep(2000); // Simulate loading time
 } catch (InterruptedException e) {
 Thread.currentThread().interrupt();
 }
 System.out.println("✅ Video loaded: " + filename);
 }
 @Override
 public void show(Media canvas) { canvas.with("video", filename);}
} 
```

RealVideo simulates loading a large file. The constructor calls load(), which takes time. If this video were loaded immediately at initialization, the application would be delayed unnecessarily.

**Lazy-loading proxy (LazyVideo):**

```java
public class LazyVideo implements Video {
 private RealVideo realVideo;
 private String filename;
 public LazyVideo(String filename) {
 this.filename = filename;
 }
 @Override
 public void show(Media canvas) {
 if (realVideo == null) {
 System.out.println("🎬 Initializing video for the first time...");
 realVideo = new RealVideo(filename);
 }
 realVideo.show(canvas);
 }
} 
```

The LazyVideo proxy delays initialization of the real video until the first call to show(). Thus the video is loaded only when it actually needs to be played. This is a classic example of **lazy loading**.

AnalogMovie shows the full orchestration:
- **Set up infrastructure:** database, phone, payment API, and email service are initialized.
- **Cast actors:** bride, groom, child, guests, and band are created.
- **Dress actors:** decorators equip the main characters with additional capabilities (phone, payment card, email, database).
- **Fill film roll:** all scenes are added to the film roll in the correct order.
- **Play the film:** the scenes are executed one after another.

Directing is the only place that knows all dependencies. No other package may access it. This guarantees that the domain remains independent and technical orchestration is isolated.

**Flow of the staging**

When the main method is executed, the entire staging runs. The console shows a lively narration:

- **Scene 1:** The groom calls the band. The phone publishes the message and the band confirms.
- **Scene 2:** The band wants to be paid. StripePayment translates Romeo’s domain pay() into the technical language execute() of the Stripe API.
- **Scene 3:** The video flashback starts. Thanks to the **Proxy Pattern**, the heavy film is loaded only in the moment the prompter provides the canvas.
- **Acts 2 to 4:** Life unfolds — from the clinic to the bedtime story — cleanly separated into acts and scenes. The further acts of the life story run one after another.

### **11. Blueprint as screenplay**
 
To ensure a domain-oriented and progressive narrative structure in code, a clear separation is required between the type of communication and the physical organization of the project.

**The guiding principles (the directing instruction)**

These principles define how actors within the system interact to preserve encapsulation and autonomy:

**Graphs as script:** The modeling of the system happens across three dimensions. The _collaboration graph_ maps the ensemble, the _construction graph_ regulates the set setup, and the _invocation graph_ describes the dialogue of objects at runtime.

**Tell Don't Ask:** Interactions happen exclusively through domain commands. An object is never asked for its state in order to make external decisions; it is directly instructed to act.

**Law of Demeter:** Actors communicate only with their immediate neighbors. This restriction of access paths guarantees loose coupling and protects the staging from semantic instability.

**Rules for the project structure (the table of contents)**

So the story remains findable for the reader, packaging follows domain instead of technical criteria:

**Domain centricity:** The package structure does not orient itself to technical layers (like _Service_ or _Repository_), but follows the hierarchical domain concepts.

**Focus on level zero:** The top package level contains only the domain core. This includes _interfaces_, _abstract classes_, _value objects_, _entities_, and the central _system interface_ as the entry point of the application. This level remains free of technical ballast.

**Separation of realization:** All concrete implementations and technical details (like _infrastructure_ and _user presentation_) are strictly separated into subordinate packages.

**Hierarchy without cycles:** A parent package may never depend on its subpackages. Cyclic dependencies must be strictly avoided to preserve a directed information flow. The use of subpackages is permitted as long as they are not on the same hierarchy level.

**Detail refinement:** Subpackages do not introduce new domain concepts. They serve only the content-level elaboration of the concepts from the parent level.

**Domain language (Ubiquitous Language):** Naming of _packages_, _classes_, and _methods_ follows the language of the real domain world, not the technical implementation.

### **12. Final word: code that tells stories**
 
To understand software as a screenplay means leaving the world of lifeless data containers and stepping onto a stage where domain language becomes visible. The strict separation of backstage technology and directing orchestration frees the domain from technical ballast. What remains is pure narration — clear, human, and comprehensible.

In the end, the cinematic structure is an emotional investment. It transforms a lifeless machine into an understandable narrative. When we replace the stress during debugging with the clarity of a stack trace that tells a story, we gain not only time, but also joy in our work. Software development is thus also the art of building systems whose story one likes to share.[ ](https://github.com/andreas-wagner-dev/object-oriented-learning-journey/blob/main/blog/picture/008_11_code_like_movie.png)

**The developer’s new role as director**

The feature-film philosophy thus goes beyond merely filling technical stencils or gluing together AI output and transforms reading code into an intuitive experience. The role of the developer changes into director and author.

So let us become storytellers and create code that not only works, but brings its own meaning precisely onto the screen.
